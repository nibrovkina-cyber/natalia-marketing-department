# The 1966 book that explains why your marketing isn't working

*Eugene Schwartz wrote five awareness levels into Breakthrough Advertising sixty years ago. Most copywriters today haven't read it. The cost of skipping it is everything.*

---

## I.

A month ago I sat with a founder who'd spent eighteen months trying to grow a B2B SaaS. Smart product, narrow market, well-funded enough to do real marketing. They'd written ten landing pages, run three ad campaigns, started a content blog, hired a designer, hired an agency, fired the agency, hired another agency. The conversion needle hadn't moved. They couldn't figure out why.

I asked them: "When someone arrives at your homepage, what do they already know?"

They paused. Then said: "What do you mean?"

That pause is the cost of skipping Schwartz.

---

## II.

Eugene Schwartz published *Breakthrough Advertising* in 1966. It's been out of print for most of its life. Original copies sell on eBay for $300 to $800. There are bootleg PDFs floating around the internet, but the reason Boron Letters and Breakthrough Advertising are still legal-grey is because almost everyone who reads them ends up making more money, and they don't want to share.

The book is dense. It's full of advertising examples from the 1950s and 1960s — vacuum cleaners, rye bread, mail-order books — that won't read as immediately useful to a 2026 SaaS founder. The first two times I tried to read it, I gave up.

The third time, I came across the chapter on awareness levels and stopped. Re-read. Read again. Then I went and rewrote every sales page I'd ever written.

Here's the entire model in one paragraph: Before you write anything, identify what your reader already knows. There are five possible levels. Each level needs a different opening, a different proof structure, a different call to action. If you write at the wrong level, your copy at best feels off, at worst feels insulting. Most copywriting fails not because the writer is bad but because the writer guessed wrong about what the reader knows.

The five levels:

1. **Most aware.** Knows you, knows your product, ready to buy. Wants only the offer. Wants only the price.
2. **Product aware.** Knows your product exists. Hasn't bought because they're not sure it solves their problem better than alternatives. Needs comparisons, proof, social validation.
3. **Solution aware.** Knows the category of solutions exists, doesn't know who to pick. Searches "best X" and "X vs Y." Needs to be educated about why your specific approach is better.
4. **Problem aware.** Knows they have a pain. Doesn't know there's a category of solutions for it. Spends time on Reddit complaining. Needs the pain named back to them precisely, then revealed to be solvable.
5. **Unaware.** Doesn't know they have a problem. Has never thought about it. Going about their day. Needs a story or a provocation that makes the problem visible to them for the first time.

That's the whole model. It looks too simple to matter. It is the most consequential framework in direct response.

---

## III.

The reason it matters is that 80% of your sales come from levels 1 and 2 — Most Aware and Product Aware. They're warm. They're already looking. The funnel is narrow at the bottom and they're already in it.

But 80% of your *audience* is on levels 3, 4, and 5 — they're cold. They don't know you. Some don't even know they have the problem.

If you spend all your content writing for levels 1 and 2 (which is what most marketers do, because writing for warm leads is easy and feels productive), you're writing for the smallest part of your market. You optimize the bottom of the funnel for the people who were already going to convert anyway. Meanwhile the entire top of the funnel — the people you have to *warm up* — never hears from you. You stay small.

If you spend all your content writing for levels 4 and 5 (which is what most thought-leaders do — the LinkedIn-essay crowd, the Twitter-thread crowd), you build a big audience but you don't sell anything. You inspire problem-aware readers who will eventually buy, but if you never write the offer copy that converts them when they're ready, they buy from the next person who shows up at the right moment.

The asymmetry is: 80% of sales / 80% of content. You have to do both, and you have to do them in different places, with different voices, on different schedules. Most marketers don't. Most marketers pick one and lose half their potential market.

---

## IV.

Here's how the levels show up across the channels you'd actually use:

**Most Aware (Level 1).** Email retargeting. Yandex.Direct or Google Ads on your brand keyword. SMS to existing clients about a new offer. Cart-abandonment sequences. The reader is hot. Your job is friction-removal: one CTA, one price, urgency if you have it. 30-60 word emails. Single-product landing pages with no nav.

**Product Aware (Level 2).** FAQ pages. Comparison pages ("X vs Y"). Long-form sales pages. Video case studies. The reader is comparing. Your job is closing objections — every paragraph addresses one specific reason they haven't bought yet. 1500-3000 word landing pages. Detailed pricing pages with FAQ blocks.

**Solution Aware (Level 3).** SEO articles. YouTube long-form. Podcasts. The reader is searching for the right *category* of solution and trying to pick. Your job is to show your specific approach is the right one — without sounding promotional. 1500-3000 word educational articles. Demo videos. Methodology essays. (This essay is for level 3 readers — people who know AI marketing exists and are deciding which approach to take seriously.)

**Problem Aware (Level 4).** Short-form social. Reddit posts. Newsletter essays where you name the pain precisely. The reader knows something is wrong. Your job is to name the pain so accurately they feel seen, then reveal the existence of a solution category they didn't know about. 300-800 word emotional pieces. Twitter threads. Personal-brand posts.

**Unaware (Level 5).** Storytelling. Long-form podcasts. Documentary YouTube. Books. The reader is just living their life. Your job is to make the problem visible to them — usually through a story about someone like them who *had* the problem and didn't know it until something went wrong. This is hardest, longest, and only works if you're patient.

The right channel matches the right level matches the right copy length matches the right CTA. Get any of these wrong and the campaign fails — not because any one piece is bad, but because the pieces don't fit together.

## V.

I'll show you what this looks like in practice with a real client — a dental clinic in Moscow, anonymized for the case study though not by much.

The clinic was running Yandex.Direct ads with the copy "Quality dentistry in central Moscow. Call now." This is *Most Aware* copy. It assumes the reader knows what dental services they want and is just looking for a clinic to call. Most of the people clicking that ad were not Most Aware. They were Problem Aware — they had a toothache, or they'd been told they need a crown, or they were anxious about a procedure they'd been postponing. The ad copy was wrong for the level.

We rewrote the ad as: *"Locked price before treatment. Free consultation. Pokrovka."* This is *Problem Aware* copy. It names the problem (the patient is afraid of being scammed on price) and offers a Solution-Aware framing of what to do (free consultation = low-commitment first step). Same ad slot, same budget. CTR went from 2.1% to 4.8%. Cost per lead halved.

But that wasn't enough on its own. The landing page the ad pointed to was still written for Most Aware visitors — straight to "book now" without the trust-building these readers needed. So we rewrote the landing too: hero with the locked-price promise, second screen with five concrete prices for common procedures, third screen with three short patient testimonials specifically about the price-locking experience, fourth screen with a free-consultation booking form. That sequence is Problem Aware → Product Aware → Most Aware, in three screens. Conversion from click to booking went from 2% to 6%.

But that wasn't enough either. The booked patients were ghosting at 68% — they'd schedule the consultation, talk themselves out of it by Thursday, no-show on Friday. The reason was a Schwartz failure: between booking and arriving, they'd slid back from Most Aware down to Problem Aware. The fear came back. They needed to be re-warmed.

So we built a 5-email welcome sequence sent on day 0, 1, 3, 5, 7. Each email moved the reader gently up a level: confirmation (Most Aware) → reassurance about anxiety (Problem Aware) → testimonials about similar cases (Solution Aware) → answers to specific questions (Product Aware) → final reminder with offer to reschedule (Most Aware). Show-up rate went from 32% to 51%.

Three weeks of work. Twelve weekly leads became forty-seven. The dental practice tripled in six weeks without changing the dentist, the team, the prices, or the building. All we did was make sure the messaging matched the level of the reader at every point of contact.

## VI.

I open-sourced this work. Twenty-one Claude Skills in a GitHub repo, MIT-licensed, free. They run on Claude Code. Each skill encodes one marketing task with the awareness-level model baked in — Claude won't write you a landing page until it's identified the audience level, won't draft an email sequence without showing the level progression, won't suggest an ad copy variant without naming which level it's targeting.

The methodology can't be skipped. ChatGPT will write you "quality treatment" copy on default settings because that's the average of internet writing about dentistry. You have to teach the AI which 1966 book to apply, what awareness level the audience is on, which specific verifiable claims to substitute for adjectives. That teaching is what the skills do. They're not prompts. They're the methodology written down once, then applied a thousand times across every client without re-explaining.

The repo is at github.com/nibrovkina-cyber/natalia-marketing-department. The hosted version with a UI launches this summer at natashabrovkina.com.

## VII.

If you're a marketer reading this, the assignment for this week is simple. Pick the last campaign you ran that didn't work the way you wanted. Open the ad copy, the landing page, the email sequence — whatever the reader actually saw. Read each one out loud. Ask yourself, at every line: what does this reader already know, and what level am I writing at?

If you've never asked that question, every campaign you've ever run has been operating with the wrong assumption baked in. You'll see it immediately. The fix is small, mechanical, and devastating. You don't need new ideas. You need to re-level the ones you already had.

If you're a founder reading this, the assignment is the same with one extra step. Don't try to do the rewrite yourself. Hire someone who has read Schwartz, or — and this is the path I'd take if I were starting over with no marketing budget — install the open-source skills, run them on your existing site, and edit the output. The skills will identify the level you're currently writing at, identify the level your audience is actually on, and rewrite the gap. You'll save eighteen months.

I lost eighteen months myself before I read Schwartz the third time. I'd rather you didn't.
